Background

Life is the most complex system we have ever encountered, and at the same time it is the most important one for us to understand. Billions of proteins work in every single cell.

Fortunately, decades of research in biochemistry have uncovered a great deal of how these proteins interact with each other to produce the complex phenotypes encoded in our DNA. With the big data explosion of the 21st century, it became possible to see this complex web of interactions for the first time in the form of networks.

Networks help us to understand how cellular systems work, but to really see the behavior of such networks in different conditions, we have to "switch them on" - use simulations to uncover the phenotype emerging from a given set of mutations, protein expression levels and treatment combinations.

We use a proprietary network simulator software which works much faster than any published simulator system, capable of analyzing the response of the intracellular signaling network in a fraction of a second.

This allows us to use the simulation data itself to train machine learning and artificial intelligence algorithms, enabling us to answer in silico some of the most important questions - , like what drug combinations could we use to treat a specific presentation of cancer, and what could be the specific determinants of resistance and toxicity in each case.

The mathematical underpinnings of the Turbine algorithm are well understood. The software has been tested to accurately identify synthetic interventions in large networks based on the network states before and after the intervention. The results obtained by the software using industry standard small-scale dynamic networks were in full agreement with published literature and clinical practice, and was shown to be both more accurate and yield richer results than other in silico methods.

Examples include the targeting of the PI3K pathway as a useful treatment for undruggable mutations in the EGFR pathway, or the targeting of the PHD protein in the case of tumors having mutations concerning the ATM/ATR DNA damage sensors or the checkpoint kinases CHK1/2.

We have also managed to identify novel methods for treatment, such as VHL kinase inhibition, being a purely protective intervention that could be able to lessen or prevent the side effects of other targeted cancer drugs.

Unsatisfied with the resolution of current dynamics-enabled signaling networks, and using our previous experience in the integration and curation of the compartmentalized protein-protein interaction network ComPPI, we have constructed a large, high-resolution dynamic signaling network which we currently use for our analyses.

We have also partnered with Oncompass, a molecular diagnostics service provider. This enables us to incorporate a constant stream of clinical data in our pipeline, making our predictions as accurate as possible - and better each day.

We also have a set of in vitro trials ongoing for pancreatic cancer cell lines based on our predictions, which will be published, allowing you to independently verify the accuracy and power of our method in your facilities.
