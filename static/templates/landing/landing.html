Combination therapies may be the best weapon against cancer, but designing them is costly and hit & miss

Due to tumour heterogeneity, single drug therapies are often ineffective. Rational combination therapies increase overall survival, improve trial success rates and provide clearer clinical benefits for payers.

Mathematical modeling has been used to screen combinations, but it cannot cope with the complexity of multi-drug interventions. Even cutting edge genomic analysis is useless, as combinations affect cancer cells in so many ways that their effects cannot be predicted based on static data.

But designing them is hard. It takes years of basic research for a breakthrough combination. Even then, costly clinical trials are needed to validate the combination in vitro and in vivo - with abysmal success rates.

Turbine combines the latest omics research with AI to predict complex interventions and their effects. Our mission is turning combination therapy design from a dark art into a streamlined, speedy process that’s part of every new drug trial.

Turbine finds combinations that work

Speed up combination therapy research

By screening combination candidates in silico, you can select the best drugs from a much larger set of candidates when designing clinical trials. As you don’t have to wait for in vitro experiments to run their course, all this happens in days instead of months.

Increase chance of approval for any compound

With several targeted therapies for certain cancer types, finding the right combination is extremely unlikely through trial and error clinical trials. But finding the right combination can shave months or years off trial time and decide a drug’s approval - authorities like the FDA fast track promising that show promising clinical benefits.

Increase patient survival significantly

Patients have been shown to survive x% longer when treated with combinations as opposed to singular therapies. Payers are inclined to cover longer treatments if progression free times increase significantly.

Extend drug lines

Not limited by a clinical trial’s necessary safety concerns or costs of in vitro experiments, Turbine can test combinations against any indication, discovering new therapeutic areas for drugs. Turbine already discovered new uses for kinase inhibitors that were only recently proposed by published research groups.
